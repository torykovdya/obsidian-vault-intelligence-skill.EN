# Zettelkasten in 10 Minutes

Everything you need to know before using the skill.

---

## What Is Zettelkasten

**Zettelkasten** (German for "slip box") is a note-taking method invented by German sociologist Niklas Luhmann.

Luhmann maintained 90,000 index cards of ideas. In his lifetime he wrote 58 books and 600 articles — alone, without the internet, without assistants.

The secret wasn't the number of notes. It was that every note was connected to others.

---

## Three Types of Notes (and only three)

### 1. Fleeting Notes
Quick captures of ideas, thoughts, observations. Like a sticky note on your fridge.

**Example:**
```
[in the shower]
I wonder if you could train an LLM on your own Obsidian notes?
```

**Rules:**
- Write fast, no editing
- Process within the day (you'll lose context otherwise)
- Most get discarded. The best become Permanent Notes.

---

### 2. Literature Notes
Notes from books, articles, videos, podcasts, talks.

**Example:**
```
Source: How To Take Smart Notes, Ahrens, p. 45
Idea: Luhmann wrote notes only in his own words. Quotes are an illusion of understanding.
My thought: so when I copy-paste text from an article, I'm not learning — I'm deceiving myself.
```

**Rules:**
- Your own words, not quotes
- Always include source and page number
- One idea = one note (not a summary of the whole book)

---

### 3. Permanent Notes
The heart of the system. Atomic ideas written so that "future you" understands without any context.

**Example of a good Permanent Note:**
```markdown
# Tags as retrieval context, not storage categories

Traditional tags answer the archivist's question: "where does this go?"
Smart tags answer the researcher's question: "when will I want to stumble on this?"

The difference is fundamental: the first organizes an archive, the second
creates an environment for insights.

Bad: #ai #notes #ideas
Good: #when-building-agents #contradiction-to-explore

Links: [[Why Archives Die]] | [[Compounding Knowledge]]
Source: Ahrens, How To Take Smart Notes
```

**Rules:**
- One idea
- Understandable without context (standalone)
- Your own words
- Linked to other notes
- Short (no longer than one screen)

## Why Connections Matter More Than Count

Imagine a vault with no links: 1,000 isolated facts.
Now imagine a vault with 200 notes, each connected to 3–5 others: 600–1,000 active connections.

The second vault is more useful. Because you can "follow a thread" — from one idea to another — until you arrive at an insight you weren't looking for.

Luhmann called this "dialogue with the system."

---

## The Key Difference from Regular Notes

| Regular notes | Zettelkasten |
|--------------|--------------|
| "Where does this go?" | "When will I want to find this?" |
| Hierarchical folders | Network of connections |
| Archive (find only what you know is there) | Environment (find what you weren't looking for) |
| Value = quantity | Value = quantity × connections |
| Grows linearly | Grows exponentially |

## Minimal Practice (start today)

**Step 1:** Create an `00-Inbox/` folder in Obsidian. All new thoughts go there.

**Step 2:** Every evening, 5 minutes: review your Inbox, pick 1–2 ideas worth keeping.

**Step 3:** Write a Permanent Note for each. Use the template from `/templates/permanent-note.md`.

**Step 4:** Add at least one connection: "this idea is similar to [[X]]" or "contradicts [[Y]]."

**Step 5:** Send a batch of notes to Claude for analysis once a week.

In 3 months you'll have 50–100 Permanent Notes. That's already material for articles, products, and courses.

## Common Misconceptions

**"I need a perfect folder structure first"**
No. Luhmann used just numbers. Folder structure is secondary. Connections are primary.

**"I need to write down everything"**
No. Ahrens: "If everything is important, nothing is." Be selective. One good note per day beats ten mediocre ones.

**"This is too complicated"**
The system is: three types of notes + one rule ("link every new note to existing ones"). That's the entire method.

**"My notes aren't smart enough"**
Luhmann started with interests. Follow your curiosity. The system will surface what you're becoming an expert in.
